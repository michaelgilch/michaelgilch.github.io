A pattern I keep seeing: people get started with AI and never really get anywhere, because they're still expecting a search engine. 

And nothing changes until that does.

When we interact with technology, we're used to it being deterministic. Computers have always been predictable.

Same input yields same output, every time. 

That's what's made computers so useful for 30+ years. You press a button, the same thing happens. You run a query, you get the same results every time. 

So when a new piece of software shows up, your brain defaults to the same assumption, even if you're not consciously aware of it.

But AI doesn't work that way, and that's what we've got to get used to. You can ask it the same thing twice and get two different but both reasonable answers. 

That's not a bug. That's the whole point. But it also means it can sometimes be flat-out wrong. That's part of the deal, and something you have to learn to work with. 

And unlearning a lifetime of "the machine is deterministic" takes some time.

But here are the keys to speeding up that process...

"Best SUV for families" is the kind of question that gets you a generic top-5 list, whether you type it into Google Search or into an AI chat.

"We've got two kids, one dog, and a tight budget. Help me figure out what actually matters when comparing these three SUVs I'm looking at" gets you something a list never could. It takes judgment, context, weighing tradeoffs... the kind of thing you'd actually ask a person, not a search bar.

Don't stop after the first response. Ask follow-up questions. Push back when something feels off. Ask it to clarify, or to summarize what you've covered. Treat it like a conversation, not a lookup. 

And talk to it about something you already know well. That way, when it gets something wrong, you'll actually catch it, and get a feel for how it handles the edges of what it knows.

This mindset shift can take a while. But if that's your goal going in, you'll start feeling the difference in about ten minutes. 

You're not trying to become an expert. You're just trying to get a feel for how the model responds, what it's good at, where it gets weird. 

Ten minutes with a clear goal like that will teach you more than hours of reading takes on AI ever will, **including this one!**
